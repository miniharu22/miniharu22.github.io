Differences in potential, One-side Junction    

## 1. Differences in potential

&nbsp;

<p align="center"><img src="/assets/images/pnjunction/19.png" width="50%" height="50%"  title="" alt=""/></p>

We learned that when different types of semiconductors are joined, **Built-in E-field** occurs in the depletion region. In other words, Electric field is generated, which means that a differenct in electric potential has occured within the Depletion region.  

The Voltage inherently generated by this difference is called **Built-in Potential ($$V_{bi}$$)**.  


&nbsp;

<p align="center"><img src="/assets/images/pnjunction/20.png" width="50%" height="50%"  title="" alt=""/></p>

This generated $$V_{bi}$$ is expressed as the difference in energy level and we can also express it as the concentration of carriers or dopants through **Boltzmann Approximation** for non-generate semiconductors.  

However, why we limit it to non-degenerate doping?  

If the doping concentration exceeds $$10^{18}cm^{-3}$$ based on the semiconductor, it is called **Degenerate Doping**. In this case, the above formulas cannot be applied because the carrier concentration cannot be calculated using the Botlzmann Approximation.  

&nbsp;


## 2. One-side Junction (Degenerately doped) 

&nbsp;

<p align="center"><img src="/assets/images/pnjunction/21.png" width="50%" height="50%"  title="" alt=""/></p>

What would happen if only one doping concentration was highly doped and degenerate doped?  

Often the case where the difference in doping concentration between both sides is very large is called **One-side Junction**. In this case, a seperate method must be applied when calculating the built-in potential.  

Earlier, we learned that in the case of degenerate doping, Boltzmann approximation cannot be used. Therefore, in the One-side Junction, $$V_{bi}$$ is calculated by approximating the difference between $$E_F$$ and $$E_i$$ in the highly doped side to half of the Bandgap($$E_G$$).
